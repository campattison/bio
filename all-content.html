About Me

About My Research

I am a Ph.D. student in Philosophy at Vanderbilt University exploring the intersection of classical philosophical traditions and artificial intelligence. My research examines how premodern conceptions of rationality can illuminate contemporary debates about AI ethics and cognition. I investigate whether large-scale AI models resemble human cognition in ways that challenge assumptions about human uniqueness, and consider what this means for moral status and our ethical frameworks. I also lead initiatives on AI applications in humanities research and teaching with Vanderbilt's RPW Center.

Research Journey

Classical Foundations

My research began with questions about cognition and reason in the Aristotelian tradition and its development in the Islamic philosophical world, examining how premodern thinkers grappled with rationality, perception, and human nature.

Bridge to Contemporary Questions

This historical investigation led to deeper questions about what cognition means—human or otherwise—and how these classical frameworks can inform our understanding of artificial intelligence.

Current Focus

Working at the intersection of classical philosophy and AI ethics, exploring how historical philosophical frameworks can help us navigate questions of moral agency, rationality, and intelligence in artificial systems.

Current Projects

Normative Dimensions of Summarization

Examining how AI systems summarize public comments and input in normatively weighty contexts, with a focus on identifying whether these systems capture or miss morally significant perspectives.

This research investigates three critical questions in AI-assisted democratic deliberation: (1) How are automated summarization systems currently used in policy contexts? (2) How are these models optimized, and what values do they prioritize? (3) What normative dimensions are overlooked, particularly high-signal, low-frequency perspectives that may hold moral significance?

Nothing Infers: AI, Human Cognition, and the Taking Condition

Exploring how both AI systems (symbolic AI and large language models) and human cognition may fail to satisfy the philosophical "Taking Condition" for reasoning.

Drawing on evidence from neuroscience and cognitive psychology, this paper argues that human cognition likely operates on similar principles to AI, particularly in its reliance on pattern recognition and statistical learning. This leads to the problematic conclusion that, under the current definition, nothing 'reasons' - suggesting we need to reconsider our definition of reasoning itself.

About the Seminar

This seminar explores what it means to be intelligent in an age of artificial minds. It examines how AI reshapes our sense of justice, power, and humanity. Join us for discussions with global experts in ethics, consciousness, and AI.

Focus and Aims

  • How does AI challenge traditional concepts of intelligence and human uniqueness?
  • What ethical dilemmas arise from the integration of AI into our daily lives?
  • How does AI reshape existing power dynamics, and what new forms of power does it create?
  • How can the humanities enrich the ongoing discourse on AI's societal implications?

Ethical Analysis Tools: My Development Work

Overview of My Ethical AI Tools

I've developed a suite of AI-powered tools for ethical analysis that bridge classical philosophical frameworks with modern AI capabilities. These tools help students and researchers systematically analyze ethical situations through different theoretical lenses.

Kantian Ethics Analyzer

My Kantian Ethics Analyzer tool extracts the underlying maxim of an action and tests it against Kant's categorical imperative. It systematically evaluates whether a maxim can be universalized without contradiction and whether it treats humanity as an end in itself.

The tool employs a carefully calibrated prompting system that guides users through formulating maxims properly and applying the universalization and humanity formulas correctly. This addresses common misconceptions in applying Kantian ethics.

Consequentialist Analysis System

I designed this tool to provide structured analysis of ethical situations through consequentialist frameworks. It identifies all stakeholders, systematically evaluates immediate and long-term consequences, and assesses aggregate welfare impacts across different populations.

The system incorporates nuanced considerations of welfare distribution and qualitative differences in outcomes that are often overlooked in simplistic consequentialist analyses.

Virtue Ethics Framework

This tool, which I developed for teaching virtue ethics, helps users identify relevant virtues and vices in ethical situations and evaluate how actions reflect and impact character development. It incorporates classical Aristotelian concepts of practical wisdom (phronesis) and flourishing (eudaimonia).

The framework is designed to highlight the context-sensitivity of virtue ethics and show how the same action might express different virtues or vices depending on the agent's character and circumstances.

Pedagogical Applications

I've implemented these tools in undergraduate philosophy courses to help students gain deeper understanding of ethical theories by seeing them applied systematically. Student feedback indicates that interacting with these tools helps clarify abstract theoretical concepts and reveals the practical differences between ethical frameworks.

The tools serve not just as analytical aids but as pedagogical devices that help students recognize the structure and reasoning patterns of different ethical traditions.

Technical Implementation

These tools are built using advanced LLM technology with carefully designed prompting structures. They rely on OpenAI's API infrastructure and can be accessed through simple web interfaces. The systems incorporate safeguards against common errors in ethical reasoning.
